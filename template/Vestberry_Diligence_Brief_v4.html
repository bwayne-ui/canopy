Executive Summary · Vestberry Acquisition Brief · April 2026
Acquisition Analysis in Progress — Diligence Requirements Being Defined
Vestberry is a VC portfolio monitoring platform based in Slovakia with $45B+ AUM across 500+ funds. The strategic case is compelling across multiple axes: it directly addresses the competitive pressure from Carta, Hanover Park, and Thunderbird gaining traction in JSQ deals; it opens significant cross-sell opportunities with existing JSQ clients; and its European relationships and presence provide a foundation for global expansion. Before advancing, Finance, Product, Engineering, and Jay Farber need to provide their list of requirements and needs to determine the buying decision. Key open items: deal economics, product integration complexity, whether our business can handle another international acquisition at this time, and leadership retention structure.

PE Expansion Cost: Vestberry is VC-native. Supporting PE workflows requires an estimated 2+ months of additional development investment

IP Not Clean: Slovakia-based contractor IP must be verified. Unresolved open-source or ownership issues must be resolved or indemnified pre-close

Strategic Thesis
Why Vestberry, Why Now
Three independent rationales — each one alone justifies consideration. All three together make this a high-priority evaluation.
1 — Closing the Competitive Gap Costing JSQ Deals Today
The Sales Opportunity Feedback log documents $8.7M in ARR directly impacted — lost or at risk — because JSQ lacked portfolio monitoring and fund/scenario modeling capabilities. Carta, Hanover Park, and Thunderbird are winning these deals. This is not a hypothetical future threat; it is happening now, across named accounts, with cited ARR. Vestberry closes the portfolio monitoring gap entirely and significantly closes the fund/scenario modeling gap. Every quarter JSQ waits is a quarter more of confirmed losses and a stronger competitive position for Carta and Hanover in the VC fund administration market.
2 — Cross-Sell Opportunity Across the Existing JSQ Client Base
JSQ's existing fund administration clients are the natural first buyers of a portfolio monitoring layer. The deal log shows that clients are already asking for this capability — and disqualifying JSQ when it isn't available. Vestberry acquired becomes a direct cross-sell motion into JSQ's book of business from day one. This is incremental revenue from a pre-existing relationship base, not a cold-start market entry. The EU cross-sell alone — Vestberry's 100+ European clients as a target base for JSQ FA services — adds a second revenue vector running in parallel.
3 — European Relationships and Global Expansion Platform
Vestberry's 104 clients across the UK (29% of ARR), Germany/DACH (25%), Benelux (10%), Nordics (9%), and APAC (9%) represent established venture industry relationships that JSQ does not have. Building equivalent EU distribution organically would take 3–5 years. These are not just clients — they are reference relationships with institutional VCs like Atomico, Dawn Capital, and Earlybird, which carry significant credibility in the European fund management community. The Slovakia-based engineering team also provides a cost-advantaged EU operating base: senior engineering talent at 40–55% of equivalent US cost, structurally improving the economics of EU market support and product development over time. This is a foundation for global expansion, not just a European foothold.
4 — Engineering Talent With Deep Industry Knowledge
Vestberry is engineering-first — but what differentiates this team is not just technical seniority, it is domain depth. These engineers and product leaders have spent years building specifically for VC fund managers: understanding portfolio data models, LP reporting requirements, fund accounting workflows, and ESG compliance needs. That industry knowledge is a gap in many engineering organisations, including areas of JSQ's own engineering effort. Even if JSQ were to build an equivalent product using AI-assisted development or alternative approaches, a domain-knowledgeable team would still be required to direct, validate, and support the result. The Director of Engineering has led teams of up to 60 at Konica Minolta, SolarWinds, and Quest Software, with direct onPrem-to-SaaS transition experience. The Head of Product brings 10+ years leading PM at AVG, Kiwi.com, Notino, and Invicti. Hiring an equivalent team in the US market would take 18–24 months at 2.5–3x the cost — and would not come with this level of private markets domain expertise embedded from day one.
